What is an EM Diagram?

Energy-Maneuverability (EM) theory was developed by fighter pilot and engineer John Boyd in the 1960s. It revolutionized how aircraft performance is understood and compared.

An EM diagram is a three-axis plot: airspeed vs. turn rate vs. load factor (G). Overlaid on this are Ps (specific excess power) contours, angle of bank lines, and turn radius information. It shows every possible performance state your aircraft can achieve and where the boundaries are:

  • Stall boundary: Where the wing runs out of lift at each G level
  • Structural limit: Where the airframe runs out of strength
  • Ps contours: Energy state—climbing, accelerating, or bleeding speed
  • Angle of bank overlay: What bank angle produces what G and turn rate
  • Turn radius: How tight you can turn at each airspeed/G combination
  • Corner velocity (Va): Where lift limit meets structural limit

Fighter pilots use EM diagrams to understand tactical performance. Now you can see the same information for your Cessna, Cirrus, or Bonanza.

AEROEDGE Exclusive

Dynamic Vmc & Dynamic Vyse

The red line in your POH is a lie—or at best, a half-truth. That published Vmc was tested under specific conditions: max gross weight, most rearward CG, sea level, critical engine windmilling, 5° bank into the good engine, takeoff power. Change any variable, and your actual Vmc changes too.

AEROEDGE calculates Dynamic Vmc in real-time. See how your minimum control speed shifts as you reduce weight, climb to altitude, or adjust configuration. This isn't a list of "factors that affect Vmc"—it's the actual calculated speed for your conditions.

The same applies to Vyse. Your best single-engine climb speed isn't fixed. Dynamic Vyse shows you the optimal speed for your actual weight and density altitude, not the sea-level-max-gross number in the book.

Why This Matters

Multi-engine training teaches "factors that affect Vmc" as a memorization exercise:

  • Weight decreases → Vmc increases
  • Density altitude increases → Vmc decreases
  • CG moves forward → Vmc decreases

But how much? Nobody tells you. AEROEDGE does. See the actual numbers, plotted on the EM diagram, so you understand your real safety margins—not abstract relationships.
